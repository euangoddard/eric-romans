Roman Chariots and Gladiators
by Eric Godard

Facts aboat chariot racing

  • The Romans likely borrowed the custom of chariot racing from the Etruscans
  • In republican and imperial times, Roman chariot races, or circenses, were staged in arenas known as circuses.
  • The centre of chariot racing in Rome was the Circus Maximus, which could seat 250,000 people
  • The chariots were made from wood and leather, making them light to handle, and aiding speed and manoeuvrability

Facts aboat gladiators

  • A gladiator usually needed to fight 15 times to win his freedom – so lots of them never escaped their violent slavery
  • Not all gladiators wanted to be free – the legendary Flamma fought 34 times, and refused his freedom on 4 separate occasions
  • The lowest class of gladiators stood no chance in the arena, and were treated with no dignity after death
  • Most celebrity gladiators wielded their swords against men – but one of the 1st-century best-loved fighters was a slayer or wild beasts
